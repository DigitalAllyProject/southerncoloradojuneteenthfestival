Southern Colorado Juneteenth Festival (6th Annual)

★ Anchor Juneteenth 2026 Events

+

The largest Juneteenth celebration in southern Colorado. Now in its sixth year, the festival was founded by One Body Entertainment, a Black-led nonprofit, to commemorate the emancipation of enslaved African Americans. ANCHOR JUNETEENTH EVENT

Denver Juneteenth Music Festival (JMF)

★ Anchor Juneteenth 2026 Events

+

One of the oldest and largest Juneteenth festivals in the United States, the Denver Juneteenth Music Festival has been held annually since 1987 along Welton Street in Five Points — historically known as the 'Harlem of the West.' The festival opens with a parade from Manual High School featuring some 5,000 marchers and 100 community groups. Two stages showcase jazz, hip-hop, R&B, and soul; 200+ vendors line the street; and a sanctioned youth boxing tournament continues a Five Points athletic tradition. The festival anchors a citywide Juneteenth weekend including the Plaza Southern Soul Party (Friday) and Juneteenth Hop bar crawl (Sunday).

Address
Historic Five Points, Welton Street between 26th & 28th Streets (epicenter at 27th & Welton)
Phone
(720) 505-3274
Dates
Saturday, June 20, 2026, ~12 PM – 8 PM

ANCHOR JUNETEENTH EVENT

Pueblo's 46th Annual Freedom Day Celebration

★ Anchor Juneteenth 2026 Events

+

The oldest continuously running Juneteenth celebration in Colorado, founded in 1978 — 43 years before Juneteenth became a federal holiday. Organized by the Juneteenth Organization of Pueblo, Inc., the celebration takes place at Ray Aguilera Park (formerly Bessemer Park) in Pueblo's historic steel-mill neighborhood, where many Black families settled after migrating from Bessemer, Alabama, to work at the Colorado Fuel & Iron mill. The day includes the Miss Juneteenth Pageant, Sweets Walk, dessert contest, BBQ and Creole food, free public swimming, and scholarship presentations.

Cultural Pioneer Monument

Fannie Mae Duncan Statue ("Everybody Welcome")

Statues, Monuments & Memorials

+

Bronze statue by sculptor Lori Kiplinger Pandy, unveiled October 26, 2019 — the first statue of an African American woman in Colorado Springs. The statue depicts Fannie Mae Duncan (1918–2005), founder of The Cotton Club (1948–1975), the city's first racially integrated nightclub. Duncan's 'Everybody Welcome' policy defied prevailing segregation by seating Black and white patrons together while hosting Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington, Count Basie, Etta James, Mahalia Jackson, and Billie Holiday. Duncan was inducted into the Colorado Women's Hall of Fame.

Address
In front of Pikes Peak Center for the Performing Arts, 190 S Cascade Ave, Colorado Springs, CO 80903

Historic Site / Neighborhood

The Carter Payne (Original Payne Chapel AME, 1897)

Historic Buildings & Neighborhoods

+

The original stone Payne Chapel AME Church building, constructed in 1897 from stone quarried in Bear Creek Canyon — the first African American church building in Colorado Springs. Designated a Colorado state historical site in 1985. From 1921 to the mid-1930s, the building served as the local headquarters of Marcus Garvey's Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA), and Garvey himself spoke here in 1922 and 1924. The building now operates as a restaurant and event space.

Address
320 S Weber St (corner of Weber & Costilla), Colorado Springs, CO 80903

Historic Site / Neighborhood

Hillside Neighborhood

Historic Buildings & Neighborhoods

+

A historically Black neighborhood southeast of downtown Colorado Springs, bounded roughly by Fountain Boulevard, I-25, and Highway 24. The 1936 federal Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) redlining map outlined Hillside in red, designating it 'hazardous' for mortgage lending — a discriminatory designation applied to most Black neighborhoods nationally. The community organized through the Hillside Neighborhood Association (1985–2012), led by Reverend Promise Lee, which won All-American City Awards in 1997. Anchored by the Hillside Community Center, Helen Hunt Campus, Living Word Baptist Church, and the Leon Young Pavilion.

Address
SE of downtown Colorado Springs, bounded roughly by Fountain Blvd (N), I-25 (W), Hwy 24 (S)

Leon Young Pavilion (South Shooks Run Park)

Parks Named for Black Figures (COS)

+

Pavilion in South Shooks Run Park named for Leon Young (1924–2004), Colorado Springs' only African American mayor. Young served on City Council from 1973 to 2001 (the longest tenure in city history at the time), as Vice Mayor from 1981 to 1997, and as Interim Mayor in 1997. The Leon Young Sports Complex and Leon Young Service Center elsewhere in the city also bear his name.

Historic Site / Neighborhood

Dearfield, Colorado

Statewide Historic Black Sites

+

The only successful all-Black agricultural colony in Colorado history, founded May 5, 1910 by Oliver Toussaint Jackson on a 320-acre homestead in Weld County. At its 1921 peak, Dearfield had approximately 300 residents, a school, two churches, a grocery, a dance pavilion, and successful crops including potatoes, sugar beets, and pinto beans. The Dust Bowl and Great Depression drove most residents away by the 1940s. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places (1995) and as a Colorado Registered Historic Landmark. The Black American West Museum stewards approximately 16 acres including Jackson's house and the Squire Brockman House. The Dearfield Dream Project (UNC's Dr. George Junne, CSU Architectural Preservation Institute, and Colorado Preservation Inc.) is leading active preservation work.

Historic Site / Neighborhood

Lincoln Hills & Winks Lodge (Winks Panorama)

Statewide Historic Black Sites

+

The only Black-owned mountain resort west of the Mississippi River during the Jim Crow era. Lincoln Hills Country Club opened in 1922 in Gilpin County's Pinecliffe area; Obrey Wendell 'Winks' Hamlet built Winks Lodge between 1925 and 1928 as the resort's signature accommodation. Performers and guests included Duke Ellington, Count Basie, Lena Horne, Langston Hughes, and Zora Neale Hurston. The resort was listed in The Negro Motorist Green Book and featured in Ebony magazine. National Historic Landmark designation. The historic district also includes Camp Nizhoni (1924–1945), a summer camp for Black girls. Currently preserved by the nonprofit Lincoln Hills Cares; access by event, program, or private tour only.

Museum / Cultural Institution

Barney Ford House Museum (Breckenridge)

Statewide Historic Black Sites

+

1882 Victorian home of Barney L. Ford (1822–1902), born enslaved in Virginia, who escaped to freedom on the Underground Railroad and became one of the wealthiest men in 19th-century Colorado. Ford ran successful hotels, restaurants, and barbershops; lobbied (successfully) to block Colorado's first attempted state constitution because it denied voting rights to Black men; and helped author Colorado's eventual statehood-enabling civil rights provisions. In 2025, the Barney Ford House was added to the National Park Service's National Underground Railroad Network to Freedom — only the second Colorado site so designated. Free guided tours Tuesday–Sunday.

1858 frontier adobe fort in Costilla County operated by History Colorado. From 1875 to 1879, Company G of the Ninth U.S. Cavalry — Buffalo Soldiers — was stationed at the fort, and the museum's exhibit 'buffalo soldiers: reVision' in the West Officer's Quarters features works by eight contemporary Black artists reinterpreting the Buffalo Soldier legacy. Buffalo Soldiers Historical Marker #190 stands at the gate. Free admission for active military and family from Memorial Day through Labor Day.
