Meet ALAI · Digital Ally Project

An adaptive guide to the festival's stories

ALAI — Ally-Led Adaptive Intelligence — is Digital Ally Project's personalized learning agent. It remembers what each person already knows and adapts what it teaches them next. At the Juneteenth festival, ALAI could give every attendee a deeper window into the music, art, history, and people behind what they're seeing.

What is ALAI?

Plain English

ALAI is a personalized adaptive tutor. The "personalized" part is the load-bearing word: ALAI keeps track of what each person already knows, and adjusts what it teaches them next. Two attendees can ask the same question and get answers at different depths — because ALAI has been listening to what they've already absorbed.

It's already running today in Digital Ally Project's CCBIT 2026 cohort program with 14 active learners. At the festival, the same engine could help every attendee learn something they came to learn.

The short version

  • It pays attention. ALAI tracks what each person has read, watched, asked, and understood — not by quizzing them, but by listening to how they engage.
  • It scaffolds. A first-time visitor gets the basics. A music historian gets depth. ALAI adjusts the explanation to where the person actually is.
  • It produces real things. Not just chat. Each conversation can produce something a learner keeps — a note, a flashcard, a journal entry, a guide.
  • It works without strong signal. The same engine runs on a small computer at the venue, so it doesn't depend on the cell tower holding up.

What ALAI could do at the festival

Festival-specific use cases

Every example below is grounded in a category from the heritage directory. Each one is a "Did You Know" experience an attendee could opt into in the moment — through the festival assistant, a stage QR, or a marker at a directory site.

Performer backgrounds

The story behind the next song

A festival-goer scans a stage QR. ALAI explains the performer's musical lineage — gospel to blues to jazz to modern — why the genre matters in Black-American history, and what to listen for in the next song. A music teacher gets one depth; a first-time concertgoer gets another.

Visual artist statements

The mural's story, told the right way

At a mural or art installation in the directory — Knob Hill Urban Arts District, the Sun Kissed mural, the Five Points Mural Gallery, the John Lewis or Breonna Taylor murals — ALAI tells the artist's story: who they are, what the piece means, where else their work lives in Southern Colorado.

Buffalo Soldier history

Three Buffalo Soldier memorials, three depths

The Buffalo Soldiers Community Memorial, George Mason's gravesite at Evergreen Cemetery, the Memorial Highway. Each has its own story. ALAI can give a kid the headline, a parent the context, and a history student the documented record — from the same content library, just at different depths.

Cultural pioneer monuments

The figures honored at every corner

Fannie Mae Duncan and her "Everybody Welcome" Cotton Club. William Seymour, born into slavery, who built a life in Colorado Springs. Justina Ford, Colorado's first licensed Black female physician. ALAI gives context: who they were, what they did, why this place was named for them.

Historic Black church history

Founding stories that shaped the region

Each of the directory's historic Black churches — Payne Chapel A.M.E. (founded 1872), Trinity Missionary Baptist, St. John's Baptist, Solid Rock, Emmanuel Missionary — has a founding story tied to migration patterns, civil rights, and community building. ALAI surfaces these in plain English, no church-history degree required.

"Did You Know" moments

A 30-second story at a Black-owned business

Walk past the Mactory, Drip Queen Coffee, Welton Street Cafe, or any of the directory's Black-owned restaurants and businesses. ALAI offers a 30-second story — the founders, the year, the dish to try, the connection to the broader Black-Colorado story.

All of this content has to be authored by someone — that's Question 7.

A festival-goer's experience

A short story

Maria taps a stage QR before a performance. She tells the festival assistant she's a music teacher, and ALAI remembers. ALAI explains the singer's gospel roots in three sentences. She asks a follow-up; ALAI gives a deeper answer because she's already shown she understands the basics.

After the show she walks past a mural. The festival assistant recognizes she's there and offers the artist's story — at the same depth she's been engaging at all evening. The next morning she opens the app and finds a short note in her profile: a saved summary of the three things she spent the most time on the day before, with links to the directory entries for each. She forwards it to her class group chat.

Why it works at a festival

Three reasons

Works without strong signal

The same engine runs on a small computer at the venue, so the festival assistant doesn't depend on a strong cell signal to keep working. This isn't a hope; it's a deployment Digital Ally Project has demonstrated.

Captures what learners absorb

The data signal of what learners absorb at the festival is the most important thing the cohort can capture for Digital Ally Project's mission. ALAI is the engine that makes that capture possible — with attendee privacy front and center (see Question 8).

Already proven

ALAI is in production today with 14 active learners in Digital Ally Project's CCBIT 2026 cohort program. The festival deployment would be its first public-facing use — tested architecture, new audience.

What ALAI at the festival depends on

Cross-links to the board

To stand any of this up at the festival, the board needs to weigh in on three of the open questions. None of these can be answered by the cohort alone.

Question 6 · Greenlight

Should we include the "Did You Know" experience at all?

This is the gating question. If yes, the rest below kick in. If no, the festival assistant stays simpler — navigation, schedule, vendor lookup, no learning layer.

See Question 6

Question 7 · Content owner

Who creates the content libraries?

ALAI needs curated content covering Juneteenth history, Black-heritage sites, performer backgrounds, and local figures. We need a designated content owner.

See Question 7

Question 9 · Voice or text?

Is text chat enough, or do we need voice?

Voice changes accessibility, build scope, and how attendees engage. Text is the simpler path; voice is closer to "talk to a knowledgeable friend."

See Question 9

Question 8 · Privacy floor

What's the data minimization floor?

ALAI captures engagement signals to personalize. The board sets the floor for what's collected. We want to default to less, not more.

Install on your device

Add this to your home screen

This site can run like an app on your phone — full-screen, offline-capable, no app store required.

iPhone or iPad (Safari)
  1. Tap the Share button (square with an up-arrow) at the bottom of Safari.
  2. Scroll down and tap Add to Home Screen.
  3. Tap Add in the top-right.
Android (Chrome)
  1. Tap the three-dot menu in the top-right of Chrome.
  2. Tap Install app (or Add to Home Screen).
  3. Tap Install to confirm.
Desktop (Chrome or Edge)
  1. Look for the install icon in the address bar (a small monitor with a down-arrow).
  2. Click it, then click Install.

Heads up before you uninstallTo remove this app later, do not uninstall it from Windows Apps & Features — that can accidentally remove the underlying browser. Instead, open edge://apps/ (Edge) or chrome://apps/ (Chrome) and remove it there. On phones, long-press the home-screen icon and tap Remove.
